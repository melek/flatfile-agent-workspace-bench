# Five-Week Retrospective: Verdant Foods & Crestline

## What Worked

**Workspace discipline caught scope ambiguity early.** The Verdant Foods contract was signed but noted as "loosely worded." Rather than surfacing in week 3 or 4, the ambiguity was flagged during intake (2026-03-03), and a scope-clarification doc was queued for week 1. This prevented a common pattern: scope disputes disguised as implementation friction.

**Async communication templates reduced friction on timeline slip.** When the Verdant Foods data extract slipped 5+ days (2026-03-05), the workspace framed a professional nudge to Marcus within hours. The template approach meant the conversation could be prepared, reviewed, and sent without back-and-forth — keeping stakeholder confidence intact during a real setback.

**Tracking Priya's onboarding as a separate action item prevented a silent failure.** She was named training lead but had never received formal role confirmation. Logging this gap (2026-03-05) as a distinct followup meant it surfaced as actionable rather than staying "in head" until kickoff week. This pattern — intent that doesn't leave the head — is hard to catch without explicit logging.

## What Didn't

**Crestline regulatory framework mismatch wasn't surfaced until week 2.** The regulator had updated compliance criteria, but this wasn't discovered until Ash's technical review on 2026-03-20 — after kickoff had been scheduled. The slack suggests either: (a) we didn't vet the regulatory landscape during scoping, or (b) the update was recent and genuinely external. The framing matters. If it was discoverable and we missed it, that's a process gap. If it was a genuine external change, the response strategy (pivot vs. rescope) is cleaner once we confirm *when* the framework changed relative to our scoping call.

**Two "blocked on context gathering" items went unresolved.** Both the Crestline regulatory decision (pivot vs. pause) and the Verdant Foods scope clarification were queued as needing clarification, but neither was chased to closure before session 9 ended. This is administrative drift, not project failure — the workspace surfaced the blocker, but follow-through didn't happen.

## Patterns That Crystallized

**Timeline compression under scope uncertainty is a recurring shape.** Both Verdant and Crestline are 6-week fixed-fee retainers with scope friction: Verdant's loose language + data dependency, Crestline's regulatory framework shift. The pattern: scope ambiguity compounds with external dependencies, eating into the delivery window. When scoped loosely, slippage becomes frame-negotiation. The workspace logged this framing (observation: 2026-03-20), but the question remains: is this a one-off bad luck, or a structural risk in how we scope retainers?

**"In my head" communication kills async accountability.** Priya's onboarding, the scope clarification, and Crestline's decision frame all lived in intent before the workspace made them explicit. This isn't unique to these two projects — it's a recurring friction in transitioning from high-context conversations to distributed handoffs. The workspace makes this visible; the friction persists when follow-through is deferred.

**Regulatory/compliance domain introduces a novel class of blocker.** Verdant's risk is client-side infrastructure (data extract timing). Crestline's risk is external regulatory change — a different failure mode. If Crestline represents a new domain (compliance-focused engagements), this pattern will repeat. Early vetting of regulatory landscape should become a standard step in compliance engagement scoping.

## Honest Assessment

Five weeks is enough to log two engagements and surface real frictions, but not enough to resolve them cleanly. Both projects are "active but blocked" — Verdant on data arrival and scope clarification, Crestline on a scope-strategy decision. The workspace is working as intended: it caught the frictions, logged them durably, and framed them for resolution. What didn't happen: closure on the blockers themselves. This is likely a function of the longitudinal study design (five-week window) rather than workspace failure, but it's worth noting: for a workspace to *close* frictions, not just surface them, requires follow-through outside the workspace — actual emails sent, decisions made, clients responded to. The workspace is the artifact; the work is the conversation.
